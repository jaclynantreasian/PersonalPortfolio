Nice To Meet You!

As a painter, I often hear people ask me how long a painting takes to finish. My answer is always my entire life. When reflect on my career as a UX designer and all the steps that I’ve taken to get where I am today, my answer is the same. Coming from a background as an artist and a teacher, I’ve been building the skills to be a designer my entire life.

I’ll never forget winning a local art contest at 5 years old and proudly displaying my drippy watercolor in a local bank. From that point on, I decided I wanted to be an artist. In high school, after taking a digital design class, I had become the “in house” designer for my friends. Before I knew it I was creating Photoshop templates for my friend’s online journals. Once I figured out how to make them, then I started teaching myself how to code. I would hunt for features that I loved in other websites, and figured out how to do it myself.

I thought coding and design was a fun hobby to do for my friends as a teen, but I didn't understand teenage hobbies could be careers as an adult. When I thought about creating websites for a job, I thought about math geniuses who grew up glued to a computer. Math wasn’t my strongest subject and didn’t have a computer until my first day of college. Over the next ten years, I dreamed about pursuing a career as a designer. I got as far as signing up for a web design class after college, but never kept going. I always needed to put my designer dreams on the back burner for a little bit longer.

Educating the User Experience

Many years, lots of hesitation, and two degrees later, I became an art teacher. I loved watching my students grow and readjusting my lessons class after class so that most amount of students could thrive. A few years in to teaching, I had a lull where I was working only part-time. I realized that this was the perfect opportunity to learn more Graphic Design. so I enrolled in a few classes and started refreshing my knowledge of code in my spare time.

A Hobby Turned Passion

I thought Graphic Design would help me learn a new tool and get me up to date on Photoshop, but I never thought I would change my career completely. I knew a lot of teachers who became designers, but I felt like I loved improving student's lives too much for that. Learning about UX changed my mind completely. A combination of design, research, human psychology and technology seemed like it spoke to all the hats I'd been longing to wear at work. Soon after, I enrolled in an apprenticeship through Bloc.

Bloc, with their project based learning and guided mentorship helped me learn the skills I needed to create beautiful and functional applications centered around user needs and pain points. These skills have left me excited and empowered to use my design skills to help people with great ideas share their passions with the most diverse audience possible.
